19th and 20th
century:




  • In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, California built several psychiatric hospitals for people with mental illness.

  • While these institutions housed and treated people, some of the treatment practices in that day were unethical and reflected misconceptions around mental illness at the time.

  • Maltreatment of people with mental illness reflected racialized and gendered stigmatizations.

  • Thus, there became a movement to change how the state responds to mental health. This included a push for community-based mental health resources instead of institutionalization.

    Late 20th century:




  • During the 50s and 60s, California defunded and reduced the populations of state psychiatric hospitals. This was in large part due to the conservative and neoliberal movements which sought budget cuts. Ronald Reagan, who was California governor at the time, signed the Lanterman Petris Short Act and closed all of the state psychiatric hospitals.

  • The Lanterman-Petris-Short Act passed in 1969, also reduced the resources available for treatment of individuals with any form of mental health disorder.

  • State psychiatric hospitals closed, but there was no plan for community-based treatment in its place. This in turn relegated a lot of mentally ill people to the streets, with no resources or treatment.

    Late 20th century
    & current time:




  • Deinstitutionalization coincided with the rise towards mass incarceration.

  • During the late 20th centuries and into the present day, the state has criminalized mental illness.

  • In turn, the arrest and jailing rates of mentally ill people has increased, also exacerbating homelessness rates for this population.

  • Movements in California and LA County specifically have sought to address these intersections between the criminalization of mental illness and the lack of resources for people struggling. Specifically in LA County, Justice LA, Reform LA Jails , and Reimagine LA are community-based coalitions seeking to decarcerate LA County and use the money that would otherwise go towards policing/jailing to instead provide mental health and other services for people.

  • Studies have supported these movements, including a recent RAND Report that 60% of mentally ill people in LA County Jails are currently eligible to be released for alternatives to incarceration.

  • In addition, movements to house people on our streets are also pushing for provision of services, given how many are struggling with mental health.
